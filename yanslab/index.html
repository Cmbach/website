Research

My research focuses on children’s social development particularly in three areas: peer relations, familial socialization, and cultural influences on children’s social development. The cultural aspect is often intertwined with peer relations and familial socialization to allow me to examine the contextual influences in the developmental processes.

Peer Relations

My research interests in peer relations include examining peer status and certain social behaviors, such as aggression and prosocial behavior. My recent research in this area involves investigating the social cognitive processes related to aggression and peer status as well as how cultural values and normative beliefs are related to aggressive behaviors among children and adolescents.

Familial Socialization

The second theme of my research is to examine familial influences on children’s social development. My research in this area concerns several aspects of familial influences, including parenting beliefs and behaviors, parent-child relationships, and interparental conflict. Specifically, I am interested in investigating how various socialization goals, parenting beliefs, and cultural values relate to parenting behaviors. I also examine how important familial processes, such as interparental conflict and parent-child interactions, work as a system to influence children’s social development. Some of my recent work involves parental knowledge and parental domain specific socialization.

A recent research line of mine also investigates gifted children’s academic and social development. Collaborating with the Talent Identification Program (TIP) at Duke University, I have examined how summer program participation in middle school longitudinally related to academic performance and social activities in high school as well as gifted adolescents’ social activities outside the classroom.

Recent Project Opportunities

  1. “Cultural values, familial factors, and children’s aggressive behaviors in China: A longitudinal study” This two-year study investigates the long-term effects of familial factors (e.g., parenting behaviors, interparental conflict) and cultural values (e.g., collectivism, social harmony) on Chinese children’s (4th and 5th graders) social development.
  2. “Adolescents’ social cognitions about relationally aggressive behaviors in the U.S. and in China.” This project investigates adolescents’ (7th and 8th graders) attributions, outcome expectancies, responses, and coping strategies about relational aggression.
  3. “Online behaviors and adjustment among adolescents and young adults: a longitudinal study.” This project investigates online aggression and prosocial behaviors among adolescents and young adults and the related social and emotional adjustment issues.
